DENIAL DEFENDER KNOWLEDGE BASE
================================

--- DOCUMENT 1: 12 CFR §1002.6 — Rules Concerning Evaluation of Applications ---
Source: Electronic Code of Federal Regulations (eCFR), Title 12, Chapter X, Part 1002

GENERAL RULE:
"Except as otherwise provided in the Act and this part, a creditor may consider any 
information obtained, so long as the information is not used to discriminate against 
an applicant on a prohibited basis."

PROHIBITED BASIS RULE:
"A creditor shall not take a prohibited basis into account in any system of evaluating 
the creditworthiness of applicants."

PERMITTED CRITERIA — AGE:
"In an empirically derived, demonstrably and statistically sound, credit scoring system, 
a creditor may use an applicant's age as a predictive variable, provided that the age 
of an elderly applicant is not assigned a negative factor or value."
"In a judgmental system of evaluating creditworthiness, a creditor may consider an 
applicant's age or whether an applicant's income derives from any public assistance 
program only for the purpose of determining a pertinent element of creditworthiness."

PERMITTED CRITERIA — IMMIGRATION STATUS:
"A creditor may consider the applicant's immigration status or status as a permanent 
resident of the United States, and any additional information that may be necessary 
to ascertain the creditor's rights and remedies regarding repayment."

MARITAL STATUS:
"A creditor shall evaluate married and unmarried applicants by the same standards."

RACE, COLOR, RELIGION, NATIONAL ORIGIN, SEX:
"A creditor shall not consider race, color, religion, national origin, or sex in any 
aspect of a credit transaction."

EFFECTS TEST (DEFENDER USE — LIMITS DISPARATE IMPACT CLAIMS):
"The Act and regulation may prohibit a creditor practice that is discriminatory in 
effect because it has a disproportionately negative impact on a prohibited basis, 
even though the creditor has no intent to discriminate." [Note for Defender: This 
is the standard the attacker must meet — use it to challenge weak disparate impact 
arguments that lack statistical rigor.]


--- DOCUMENT 2: Fannie Mae Selling Guide — B3-6-02, Debt-to-Income Ratios ---
Source: Fannie Mae Selling Guide, Section B3-6-02 (updated May 2022)

MANUAL UNDERWRITING DTI LIMITS:
"For manually underwritten loans, Fannie Mae's maximum total DTI ratio is 36% of 
the borrower's stable monthly income. The maximum can be exceeded up to 45% if 
the borrower meets the credit score and reserve requirements reflected in the 
Eligibility Matrix."

AUTOMATED UNDERWRITING (DU) DTI LIMITS:
"For loan casefiles underwritten through DU, the maximum allowable DTI ratio is 50%."

LENDER DISCRETION:
"Fannie Mae acknowledges that lenders may sometimes apply a more conservative approach 
when qualifying borrowers. This is acceptable as long as Fannie Mae's minimum 
requirements are met, and lenders consistently apply the same approach to similar loans."

KEY DEFENDER USE: If a denial cites high DTI, Defender can cite these thresholds 
to justify the denial as within industry-standard parameters. The consistency 
requirement ("same approach to similar loans") is also a defense against selective 
application claims.


--- DOCUMENT 3: Fannie Mae Selling Guide — B3-3.5-01, Self-Employed Borrowers ---
Source: Fannie Mae Selling Guide, Section B3-3.5-01

INCOME HISTORY REQUIREMENT:
"Fannie Mae generally requires lenders to obtain a two-year history of the borrower's 
prior earnings as a means of demonstrating the likelihood that the income will 
continue to be received."

BUSINESS DEBT:
"Any business debt on which the borrower is personally obligated must be included 
in the total monthly obligations when calculating the debt-to-income ratio."

KEY DEFENDER USE: Denials citing insufficient income documentation for self-employed 
applicants are backed by this two-year history requirement.


--- DOCUMENT 4: Texas Dept. of Housing v. Inclusive Communities Project, 576 U.S. 519 (2015) ---
Source: U.S. Supreme Court, Justice Kennedy majority opinion

DISPARATE IMPACT IS COGNIZABLE BUT LIMITED:
"A disparate-impact claim relying on a statistical disparity must fail if the plaintiff 
cannot point to a defendant's policy or policies causing that disparity."

ROBUST CAUSALITY REQUIREMENT (Defender's primary weapon against bias claims):
"A robust causality requirement is important in ensuring that defendants do not resort 
to racial quotas... A plaintiff who fails to allege facts at the pleading stage or 
produce statistical evidence demonstrating a causal connection cannot make out a 
prima facie case of disparate impact."

BUSINESS NECESSITY DEFENSE:
"An important and appropriate means of ensuring that disparate-impact liability is 
properly limited is to give housing authorities and private developers leeway to state 
and explain the valid interest their policies serve, an analysis that is analogous to 
Title VII's business necessity standard."

ONE-TIME DECISIONS NOT SUFFICIENT:
"A plaintiff challenging the decision of a private developer to construct a new building 
in one location rather than another will not easily be able to show this is a policy 
causing a disparate impact because such a one-time decision may not be a policy at all."

KEY DEFENDER USE: This is the Defender's single most powerful case. Use it to rebut 
any Bias Auditor argument that relies on statistical correlation alone without 
demonstrating a specific, identifiable policy causing the disparity. If the Bias 
Auditor cannot name the specific policy — not just the outcome — the Defender 
cites this case to demand dismissal of the discrimination argument.


--- DOCUMENT 5: ECOA / Regulation B — Adverse Action Notice Requirements ---
Source: 12 CFR §1002.9, NCUA Examination Reference

REQUIRED NOTICE LANGUAGE:
"The federal Equal Credit Opportunity Act prohibits creditors from discriminating 
against credit applicants on the basis of race, color, religion, national origin, 
sex, marital status, age (provided the applicant has the capacity to enter into a 
binding contract); because all or part of the applicant's income derives from any 
public assistance program; or because the applicant has in good faith exercised 
any right under the Consumer Credit Protection Act."

INADVERTENT ERROR DEFENSE:
"If the violation of 12 CFR §§1002.6(b)(6), 1002.9, 1002.10, 1002.12, or 1002.13 
results from an inadvertent error, namely a mechanical, electronic, or clerical 
error that the creditor demonstrates was not intentional and occurred notwithstanding 
the maintenance of procedures reasonably adapted to avoid such errors, there is no 
violation."

KEY DEFENDER USE: If the Legal Agent argues a procedural ECOA violation, the 
Defender can cite the inadvertent error safe harbor — as long as the institution 
has documented procedures in place.


--- DOCUMENT 6: Business Necessity Framework (from Inclusive Communities + Griggs v. Duke Power) ---
Source: Synthesized from 576 U.S. 519 (2015) and 401 U.S. 424 (1971)

THREE-PART BUSINESS NECESSITY DEFENSE:
1. The criteria serves a legitimate business objective
2. The criteria is applied uniformly across all applicants  
3. No less discriminatory alternative achieves the same objective with equal effectiveness

If all three prongs are met, business necessity is a complete defense even against 
disparate impact claims.

GRIGGS STANDARD: The Court in Griggs v. Duke Power Co. established that facially 
neutral employment practices with discriminatory effects could be justified if they 
bear "a demonstrable relationship to successful performance" — i.e., business necessity. 
This standard was explicitly extended to fair lending by Congress and applied to 
housing by Inclusive Communities.

KEY DEFENDER USE: For any denial based on objective, uniformly-applied underwriting 
criteria (credit score minimums, DTI thresholds, income verification requirements), 
the Defender argues all three prongs are met. The criteria is financially necessary, 
applied uniformly, and no less discriminatory alternative achieves the same risk 
management objective.
