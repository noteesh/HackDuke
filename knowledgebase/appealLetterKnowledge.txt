APPEAL LETTER WRITER KNOWLEDGE BASE
=====================================

--- DOCUMENT 1: ECOA Adverse Action Notice Specificity Standard ---
Source: 12 CFR §1002.9(b)(2), Regulation B Official Commentary

WHAT CONSTITUTES A SPECIFIC REASON:
"The statement of reasons must be specific and indicate the principal reason(s) 
for taking adverse action."

"The specific reasons disclosed must relate to and accurately describe the 
factors actually considered or scored by a creditor."

"Statements that the adverse action was based on the creditor's internal 
standards or policies or that the applicant failed to achieve a qualifying 
score on the creditor's credit scoring system are insufficient."

WHAT THE CREDITOR MUST DISCLOSE:
"The creditor must disclose the actual reasons for denial (for example, 
'age of automobile') even if the relationship of that factor to predicting 
creditworthiness may not be clear to the applicant."

"If the applicant's credit history reveals delinquent credit obligations 
and the application is denied for that reason, to satisfy §1002.9(b)(2) 
the creditor must disclose that the application was denied because of the 
applicant's delinquent credit obligations." — meaning a general category 
is insufficient; the specific derogatory item must be named.

"If the reasons listed on the forms are not the factors actually used, a 
creditor will not satisfy the notice requirement by simply checking the 
closest identifiable factor listed."

ALGORITHMIC DECISIONS — NO EXCEPTION:
"Whether a creditor is using a sophisticated machine learning algorithm or 
more conventional methods to evaluate an application, the legal requirement 
is the same: creditors must be able to provide applicants against whom 
adverse action is taken with an accurate statement of reasons."

"ECOA and Regulation B do not permit creditors to use complex algorithms 
when doing so means they cannot provide the specific and accurate reasons 
for adverse actions."

"A creditor's lack of understanding of its own methods is not a cognizable 
defense against liability." (CFPB Circular 2022-03)

KEY USE IN APPEAL LETTER:
When the denial uses vague reasons like "debt-to-income ratio exceeds 
acceptable threshold" without stating the threshold or the applicant's 
actual ratio, cite §1002.9(b)(2) and CFPB Circular 2022-03 directly. 
Demand the specific actual reason — including the threshold applied, 
the applicant's measured ratio, and which algorithmic factors drove 
the decision.


--- DOCUMENT 2: ECOA Private Right of Action and Remedies ---
Source: 15 U.S.C. §1691e

WHAT THE APPLICANT CAN DEMAND:
Any creditor who fails to comply with ECOA is liable for:
- Actual damages (the financial harm from the denial)
- Punitive damages up to $10,000 in individual actions
- Attorney's fees and court costs

STATUTE OF LIMITATIONS:
5 years from date of violation.

REGULATORY REFERRAL PATHWAY:
If the CFPB cannot obtain compliance, it is authorized to refer the matter 
to the Attorney General for civil action.

KEY USE IN APPEAL LETTER:
Reference §1691e in the closing paragraph when stating consequences of 
non-response. Do not threaten litigation — state the statutory framework 
and let the institution draw the conclusion. Example language:
"Please be advised that under 15 U.S.C. §1691e, failure to comply with 
ECOA's adverse action requirements exposes the institution to civil 
liability including actual and punitive damages."


--- DOCUMENT 3: CFPB Circular 2022-03 — Algorithmic Adverse Action ---
Source: CFPB Circular 2022-03, consumerfinance.gov/compliance/circulars/
circular-2022-03

THE CORE HOLDING:
"ECOA and Regulation B do not permit creditors to use complex algorithms 
when doing so means they cannot provide the specific and accurate reasons 
for adverse actions."

"A creditor cannot justify noncompliance with ECOA and Regulation B's 
requirements based on the mere fact that the technology it employs to 
evaluate applications is too complicated or opaque to understand."

"No factor that was a principal reason for adverse action may be excluded 
from disclosure."

KEY USE IN APPEAL LETTER:
Cite this Circular directly when the denial comes from an "Automated 
Decisioning Department" or similar algorithmic source. The institution 
cannot hide behind the complexity of its own system. The appeal letter 
demands the actual principal factors — not the checklist approximation.


--- DOCUMENT 4: CFPB Circular 2023-03 — Proper Use of Sample Forms ---
Source: CFPB Circular 2023-03, federalregister.gov/documents/2024/04/17/
2024-08003

THE CHECKLIST PROHIBITION:
"Creditors may not rely on the checklist of reasons provided in the sample 
forms to satisfy their obligations under ECOA if those reasons do not 
specifically and accurately indicate the principal reason(s) for the 
adverse action."

"Nor, as a general matter, may creditors rely on overly broad or vague 
reasons to the extent that they obscure the specific and accurate reasons 
relied upon."

SPECIFICITY EXAMPLE FROM CFPB:
"If a complex algorithm results in a denial of a credit application due 
to an applicant's chosen profession, a disclosure that the applicant had 
'insufficient projected income' or 'income insufficient for amount of 
credit requested' would likely not suffice."

KEY USE IN APPEAL LETTER:
When the denial uses standard checklist language ("insufficient credit 
history," "debt-to-income ratio"), this Circular establishes that 
checklist language does not satisfy ECOA if it does not reflect the 
actual algorithmic factors. The appeal demands the actual factors.


--- DOCUMENT 5: CFPB Complaint Filing Process ---
Source: consumerfinance.gov/complaint (updated February 2026)

HOW TO FILE:
- Online: consumerfinance.gov/complaint (7-10 minutes)
- Phone: (855) 411-CFPB (2372), toll-free, 180+ languages
- Mail: available for those without internet access

WHAT HAPPENS AFTER FILING:
- CFPB forwards complaint to institution within 1 business day
- Institution must respond within 15 calendar days
- Final response required within 60 calendar days
- Consumer receives company response and can provide feedback
- Complaints are shared with state and federal agencies

WHAT TO INCLUDE:
- What happened (key details and dates)
- What you think would be a fair resolution
- Supporting documents (up to 50 pages)
- Dollar amounts and communications with the company

KEY USE IN APPEAL LETTER:
Include CFPB complaint filing as a stated next step in the appeal letter. 
The CC line to CFPB signals to the institution that a regulatory complaint 
is already in progress — this creates significant institutional incentive 
to respond substantively rather than ignore the letter.


--- DOCUMENT 6: Formal Appeal Letter Structure Standards ---
Source: Synthesized from CFPB guidance, Regulation B Appendix C model 
notices, and consumer rights attorney practice standards

REQUIRED ELEMENTS OF AN EFFECTIVE APPEAL LETTER:

1. OPENING — STATE THE DISPUTE CLEARLY:
Identify the denial by date, institution, and application type. 
State that you are formally appealing. Do not apologize or hedge.

2. LEGAL VIOLATIONS — CITE SPECIFICALLY:
Name each statutory violation by section. Cite the specific language 
from the regulation that was violated. Connect each violation to a 
specific element of the denial letter.

3. FACTUAL ARGUMENTS — CITE THE DEFENDER'S FAILURES:
Present only the arguments the Denial Defender could not rebut. 
Frame each argument as a factual challenge to the institution's 
stated basis — not as a request for sympathy.

4. REMEDY DEMANDED — BE SPECIFIC:
Demand one of the following clearly:
- Full reconsideration of the application
- Provision of the specific algorithmic factors used
- Written explanation of the actual DTI threshold applied
- Reversal of the denial decision
Set a 30-day deadline. Reference 12 CFR §1002.9 as the standard 
for response timing.

5. CONSEQUENCES OF NON-RESPONSE:
State that failure to respond will result in:
- CFPB complaint filing (consumerfinance.gov/complaint)
- State AG complaint if applicable
- Potential civil action under 15 U.S.C. §1691e

6. CC LINE:
Always CC CFPB if ECOA/FCRA violations are present.
CC state AG if state-specific consumer protection violations are present.
CC DOJ Civil Rights Division if discrimination findings are present.

TONE STANDARD:
Formal, authoritative, and factual. Never aggressive, never apologetic. 
The letter speaks as an equal to the institution — not as a supplicant. 
Every claim is backed by statute or regulation. Every demand is grounded 
in legal obligation, not preference.
