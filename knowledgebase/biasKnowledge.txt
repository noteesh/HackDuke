BIAS AUDITOR KNOWLEDGE BASE
=============================

--- DOCUMENT 1: ECOA / 12 CFR §1002.6 — Prohibited Bases ---
Source: eCFR, Title 12, Chapter X, Part 1002, Section 1002.6

PROHIBITED BASES UNDER ECOA:
"A creditor shall not take a prohibited basis into account in any system of evaluating 
the creditworthiness of applicants."

Prohibited bases are: race, color, religion, national origin, sex, marital status, 
age (provided applicant has capacity to contract), public assistance income, and 
good faith exercise of rights under the Consumer Credit Protection Act.

EFFECTS TEST — DISPARATE IMPACT:
"The Act and regulation may prohibit a creditor practice that is discriminatory in 
effect because it has a disproportionately negative impact on a prohibited basis, 
even though the creditor has no intent to discriminate. Congressional intent that 
this doctrine apply to the credit area is documented in the Senate Report that 
accompanied H.R. 6516, No. 94-589, pp. 4-5."

KEY USE: Even facially neutral denial criteria (DTI, credit score thresholds, 
income type) can violate ECOA if they produce a disparate impact on a protected 
class AND the institution cannot justify them by business necessity.


--- DOCUMENT 2: CFPB BISG Proxy Methodology ---
Source: CFPB, "Using Publicly Available Information to Proxy for Unidentified Race 
and Ethnicity" (September 2014, updated April 2024)
URL: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/data-research/research-reports/using-publicly-
available-information-to-proxy-for-unidentified-race-and-ethnicity/

THE CFPB'S OWN TOOL FOR DETECTING PROXY DISCRIMINATION:
"In conducting fair lending analysis in both supervisory and enforcement contexts, 
the Bureau's Office of Research and Division of Supervision, Enforcement, and Fair 
Lending rely on a Bayesian Improved Surname Geocoding (BISG) proxy method, which 
combines geography and surname-based information into a single proxy probability 
for race and ethnicity."

HOW IT WORKS:
The CFPB uses applicant surnames cross-referenced against U.S. Census surname data 
(167,409 surnames as of 2017 update) to calculate the probability that an applicant 
belongs to a particular racial/ethnic group. Geography (zip code, census tract) is 
combined with surname via Bayesian analysis to produce a probability score.

KEY USE — SURNAME AS PROXY SIGNAL:
"One method used by lenders to check the probability that an applicant is Hispanic 
or Asian is to use the last name database published by the Census Bureau, in which 
the Census Bureau reports, by race and national origin, the percentage of individuals 
with a given surname." (CFPB, Preventing Illegal Discrimination in Auto Lending)

AUDIT INSTRUCTION: When the applicant has a surname associated with a specific 
racial or ethnic group, flag this as a proxy discrimination signal. The CFPB itself 
uses this exact methodology to detect discrimination. If the denial correlates with 
a high-probability surname, it is a valid signal under the CFPB's own standards.

GEOGRAPHY AS PROXY:
"Another method to proxy for race and national origin uses the demographics of the 
census geography (e.g., census tract or block group) in which an individual's 
residence is located, and assigns probabilities about the individual's race or 
national origin based on the demographics of that area."

KEY USE: If a zip code or address is present in the denial, flag geographic proxy 
discrimination. The CFPB treats geography as a proxy for race in every fair lending 
examination.


--- DOCUMENT 3: Interagency Fair Lending Examination Procedures ---
Source: Federal Reserve / FFIEC, Interagency Fair Lending Examination Procedures
URL: https://www.federalreserve.gov/boarddocs/caletters/2009/0906/09-06_attachment.pdf

THREE FORMS OF LENDING DISCRIMINATION:

1. OVERT DISCRIMINATION:
"There is overt evidence of discrimination when a lender openly discriminates on a 
prohibited basis."

2. DISPARATE TREATMENT:
"Disparate treatment occurs when a lender treats a credit applicant differently 
based on one of the prohibited bases. It does not require any showing that the 
treatment was motivated by prejudice or a conscious intention to discriminate."

3. DISPARATE IMPACT:
"The fact that a policy or practice creates a disparity on a prohibited basis is 
not alone proof of a violation. When an Agency finds that a lender's policy or 
practice has a disparate impact, the next step is to seek to determine whether 
the policy or practice is justified by business necessity. The justification must 
be manifest and may not be hypothetical or speculative."

LESS DISCRIMINATORY ALTERNATIVE STANDARD:
"Even if a policy or practice that has a disparate impact on a prohibited basis 
can be justified by business necessity, it still may be found to be in violation 
if an alternative policy or practice could serve the same purpose with less 
discriminatory effect."

KEY USE: This is the Bias Auditor's procedural backbone. Use the three-part 
framework to classify every finding. The "less discriminatory alternative" 
standard is particularly powerful — even if the Denial Defender justifies the 
criteria by business necessity, the Auditor can counter that alternative 
criteria exist with less discriminatory effect.

ALGORITHMIC LENDING FLAG:
"This requirement [to explain reasons for adverse action] is especially important 
with the growth of advanced algorithms and personal consumer data in credit 
underwriting." (CFPB Fair Lending Report 2023)
"Vast troves of consumer sensitive data are fueling highly complex black box 
algorithms in lending." (CFPB 2023 Fair Lending Report)

KEY USE: If the denial letter comes from an "Automated Decisioning Department" 
or references algorithmic review with no human oversight, flag this explicitly. 
The CFPB has stated this is a heightened risk area requiring enhanced scrutiny.


--- DOCUMENT 4: CFPB Fair Lending Report 2023 ---
Source: CFPB Fair Lending Report FY 2023, released June 2024
URL: https://files.consumerfinance.gov/f/documents/cfpb_fair-lending-report_fy-2023.pdf

ALGORITHMIC MODELS UNDER SCRUTINY:
"The CFPB directed institutions to test credit scoring models for prohibited basis 
disparities and to require documentation of considerations the institutions will 
give to how to assess those disparities against the stated business needs."

"Institutions were directed to develop a process for the consideration of a range 
of less discriminatory models."

"Institutions were directed to test and validate the methodologies used to identify 
principal reasons in adverse action notices required under ECOA and Regulation B."

ENFORCEMENT TREND:
"The FDIC, NCUA, Federal Reserve, and CFPB referred 23 suspected cases of ECOA 
discrimination violations to the Justice Department in 2022 — up 91% from 2020. 
Cases included discrimination on the basis of national origin, sex, public 
assistance payments, race, marital status, and age."

KEY USE: Use the 91% referral increase to establish that algorithmic discrimination 
is an active enforcement priority, not a theoretical concern. If the denial is 
algorithmic and the applicant has a surname or geography with demographic signal, 
this is precisely the enforcement pattern CFPB is actively investigating.


--- DOCUMENT 5: Known Proxy Variables for Discrimination ---
Source: Synthesized from CFPB Proxy Methodology Report (2014), 
Interagency Fair Lending Examination Procedures, and CFPB enforcement actions

ESTABLISHED PROXY VARIABLES AND THEIR PROTECTED CLASS CORRELATIONS:

SURNAME → RACE / NATIONAL ORIGIN:
The CFPB uses Census Bureau surname data to assign racial/ethnic probability 
scores to applicants. Surnames with high Hispanic probability include: 
Martinez, Garcia, Rodriguez, Lopez, Hernandez, Gonzalez, Perez, Sanchez, 
Torres, Ramirez. Surnames with high Asian probability include: Chen, Wang, 
Kim, Patel, Singh, Lee, Zhang, Liu, Nguyen, Park.

GEOGRAPHY / ZIP CODE → RACE:
Census tract and zip code demographics are used by the CFPB as a proxy for 
race in every fair lending examination. A denial in a zip code with high 
minority population concentration is a recognized proxy signal.

INCOME TYPE (PUBLIC ASSISTANCE) → RACE / SEX / MARITAL STATUS:
12 CFR §1002.6 explicitly prohibits using public assistance income as a 
denial criterion. This criterion correlates with race (Black and Hispanic 
recipients are statistically overrepresented), sex (women are 
statistically overrepresented), and marital status (single-parent households).

GIG / CONTRACTOR INCOME → SEX / RACE:
Gig economy income documentation requirements that exceed standard W-2 
requirements can have disparate impact on racial minorities and women, 
who are statistically overrepresented in gig work. 
(Source: BLS Current Population Survey data)

"INSUFFICIENT CREDIT HISTORY" → RACE / NATIONAL ORIGIN:
The CFPB's 2015 report "Data Point: Credit Invisibles" found that 
26 million Americans are credit invisible, and this status is 
disproportionately concentrated among Black, Hispanic, and immigrant 
populations. Using thin-file status as a disqualifying criterion without 
considering alternative credit data has recognized disparate impact.

DTI THRESHOLDS BELOW REGULATORY MAXIMUM → POTENTIAL DISPARATE IMPACT:
Lenders who apply DTI thresholds below Fannie Mae's published maximums 
without documented business necessity create potential disparate impact 
exposure. The more conservative the internal threshold vs. the regulatory 
floor, the stronger the disparate impact signal.


--- DOCUMENT 6: CFPB Circular 2023-03 — Adverse Action Notice Requirements ---
Source: CFPB Circular 2023-03
URL: https://www.consumerfinance.gov/compliance/circulars/circular-2023-03-adverse-
action-notification-requirements-and-the-proper-use-of-the-cfpbs-sample-forms-
provided-in-regulation-b/

ALGORITHMIC ADVERSE ACTION NOTICES:
"Creditors may not simply use checklist-style forms if they do not reflect the 
actual reason for the denial of credit or other adverse action. This requirement 
is especially important with the growth of advanced algorithms and personal 
consumer data in credit underwriting."

"The legal requirement to explain the reasons for adverse actions helps improve 
consumers' chances for future credit and protects consumers from illegal 
discrimination and serves an educational role, allowing consumers to understand 
the reasons for a creditor's action and take steps to improve their credit status 
or rectify mistakes."

KEY USE: If the denial states generic reasons like "did not meet our criteria" 
or "based on your credit profile" without specificity, this violates the adverse 
action notice requirement AND is a recognized discrimination concealment risk. 
Vague reasons prevent consumers from detecting the true discriminatory basis.


--- DOCUMENT 7: Texas Dept of Housing v. Inclusive Communities (2015) ---
Source: 576 U.S. 519, Justice Kennedy majority opinion

DISPARATE IMPACT REQUIRES A SPECIFIC POLICY — NOT JUST AN OUTCOME:
"A disparate-impact claim relying on a statistical disparity must fail if the 
plaintiff cannot point to a defendant's policy or policies causing that disparity."

WHY THIS MATTERS FOR THE BIAS AUDITOR:
This cuts both ways. The Bias Auditor must identify the SPECIFIC policy causing 
the disparity — not just note a correlation. For the sample denial:
- The specific policy is: using automated credit scoring without alternative 
  data inputs for thin-file applicants
- The disparity it produces: thin-file status disproportionately affects 
  Hispanic and Black applicants per CFPB's own Credit Invisibles report
- The causal chain: automated system → thin file disqualification → disparate 
  impact on protected class

This is a complete prima facie disparate impact argument that meets the 
Inclusive Communities causality standard.
